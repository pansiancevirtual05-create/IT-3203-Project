The History of HTML

Let's first understand its history by looking at the person who started it. It was made and introduced by Sir Tim Berners-Lee in 1991. The idea for him, that made him come up with it, I read, was because he and these other scholars all over the world would meet up at CERN [European Organization for Nuclear Research]. All of this traveling to meet in a certain area would waste time. He started thinking about the idea of a place on the internet where they would meet and share their ideas, collaborate, view documents, and texts. He thought of HTML. He actually built the very first version of this on a NeXT computer, which was a machine made by Steve Jobs after he was fired from Apple.

The first website ever made was very simple. It was just a page of text with hyperlinks explaining what the World Wide Web was. It didn't have images or videos, just the ability to link one page to another. This simple act of linking was revolutionary because it meant knowledge didn't have to be stuck in one computer; it could be connected globally. During that period, HTML wasn't something out of the blue; it was borrowed from SGML [Standard Generalized Markup Language]. This is important because SGML was the OG language used by the military and government. It was an international standard, but it was very complex, strict, and hard to use. Berners-Lee’s thought was to take the basic tag structure of SGML but throw away the strict rules. He made HTML an easier version that was also forgiving because if you made a mistake in the code, the page would still try to load. Because most people in the technical field had already used SGML, HTML was very easy to learn, and its flexibility allowed it to be taken on.
